DNS domain verification

For non-passive scan modes (intrusive, custom, destructive, etc.), SecureOps may require proof that you control the DNS zone of the target site. You prove this by publishing a unique TXT record provided by the app — similar to certificate issuance or search-console verification.

This page is an end-user overview. The full technical specification (API, database, environment variables) lives in the repository: docs/VERIFICATION-AUTORISATION.md.

1. Why this check exists

Active scans send requests that resemble offensive testing. Requiring DNS control on the target domain reduces abuse against sites the user is not entitled to test.

Audit authorisation

DNS verification does not replace a contractual or legal pentest authorisation: only test applications you are allowed to assess.

2. When it applies

  • Passive scan: DNS verification is not required.
  • All other modes (intrusive, custom, destructive, etc.): it is mandatory — the scan cannot start until the domain derived from the URL is verified for your account.

3. Steps in the Scanner hub

  1. Enter the URL to scan for a scan mode other than passive.
  2. In the Domain verification (DNS) section, click Generate DNS instructions. A secret and the TXT host to create are shown.
  3. At your DNS provider (registrar, Cloudflare, etc.), create a TXT record with exactly the displayed name and value.
  4. Wait for propagation (often a few minutes; sometimes longer).
  5. Return to SecureOps and click Verify TXT. The service queries public DNS: if any returned string matches the expected secret, the domain is recorded for your account.

You can retry verification without regenerating the secret while the pending challenge is still valid.

4. Expected TXT record

  • Type: TXT
  • Name / host: _secureops-verify as a subdomain of the domain to prove (e.g. _secureops-verify.example.com)
  • Value: the token shown by SecureOps (e.g. prefix secureops-verify- followed by random characters)

Multiple TXT records on the same name are allowed: verification succeeds if at least one value matches the expected token.

5. Which “domain” is used?

From the URL, the system derives the registrable domain (often the eTLD+1, e.g. example.com for www.example.com). That is the domain you must verify and that appears in your list of verified domains.

If policy requires proving a specific host (e.g. app.client.com), the TXT name will be _secureops-verify.app.client.com — follow the instructions shown after generation.

6. After verification

  • Verification is stored with an expiry date (renewal may be required depending on configuration).
  • A given domain can be tied to only one account at a time on the platform.
  • Scheduled non-passive scans may also be skipped or denied if the domain is not verified, under the same rule.

7. Limits and good practices

  • Public resolvers may take time to see your TXT: on failure, check the zone and retry.
  • Do not share the token outside your team authorised to manage DNS.
  • For technical integration (internal assert, scan-service, Postgres tables), see the developer documentation in the repository.
